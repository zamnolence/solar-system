The Vacuum of Space

First of all, let's get rid of a misconception. When you think of a vacuum, one generally thinks of something absolute empty with a lack of air. However, there really isn't such a thing as a perfectly empty space. One fun way to find out is to try sucking all the air out of an empty bottle. Even though you think you could do it perfectly, you could still never get it empty. There would still be things like vacuum energy, gravity, and dark matter, which can't be sucked out [1], [2].

The same idea applies to the outer space (the space outside the atmosphere of planets and stars). When we say the outer space is a vacuum or is empty, we actually mean that outer space is nearly empty or almost a perfect vacuum. In reality, the space is full of cosmic voids, which are filled with gas, dust, particles, cosmic rays, radiation, gravity, electromagnetic fields, dark matter, and dark energy. Even in the interstellar space, there are still a few hydrogen atoms in every cubic meter [1], [3].

So, what is the vacuum of space? By definition, a vacuum is a space entirely devoid of matter, in which matter has been partially removed so that the space exerts less pressure than the atmosphere [4].

What causes the outer space to be nearly vacuum? Well, the short answer is that the gravity causes it [1]. Every particle of matter, no matter how small, exerts a gravitational attraction on all other particles of matter. Starting from countless gas atoms, even though the gravitational force is weak, they will pull on each other and form into stars. Under the gravitational force, the gas atoms of these stars were fused to form the heavier elements, condensed to form small clouds and rocks, and eventually formed into asteroids, moons, and planets [1]. The outer space is nearly empty because most of the matter has congregated into an asteroid, planet, moon, or star. This emptiness results in an extremely low density and pressure, which creates the nearly perfect vacuum in space [1], [2].

Another misconception is that unprotected exposure to the vacuum of space leads to instantaneous death. In reality, people are likely to survive for a couple of minutes with a few ill effects [2], [4], [5]. However, long-time exposure to the vacuum of space is still ethal, where the primary cause of death is the sudden decompression in the vacuum [6]. Under extremely low pressure, the expansion of air in a person's lungs is likely to cause lung rupture and death unless that air is immediately exhaled [5]. So, if you are thinking about holding your breath or inhaling deeply when the pressure drops, don't do it!

Decompression can also lead to a fatal condition known as ebullism, where all liquid in people's body is vaporised [5]. At the minimum, ebullism will cause gross swelling and bruising; at worst, it is likely to cause blockage in a blood vessel [6].

Another factor that affects the amount of time a human could survive in a full vacuum is an oxygen supply [6]. Knowing that we shouldn't hold our breath, the remaining oxygen will also diffuse out of the bloodstream when the lungs are exposed to a vacuum. This leads to a condition called hypoxia, or oxygen deprivation [6]. Within 15 seconds, deoxygenated blood begins to be delivered to the brain, which results in unconsciousness. Experimental results and training accidents suggest that an individual could survive for a few minutes in a vacuum while unconscious, but not much longer [6].

So, when it comes to exposure to the interstellar medium, you could manage to survive at least for a couple of minutes, albeit very uncomfortable. This is why spacewalks are usually done with two or more spacewalkers, and continuous communication is essential. If someone is having a problem, it is best to quickly send the message and get back to the station [5].
