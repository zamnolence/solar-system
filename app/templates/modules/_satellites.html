Satellites


When we say “satellite”, it generally refers to two things: natural satellites and artificial (man-made) satellites. These satellites are known to orbits a planet or a star [1]. We will briefly go through both in this section.



Natural Satellites

A natural satellite is a moon, a planet, or an astronomical body that orbits a larger planet or star [1]. These astronomical bodies are held in orbit by the gravitation of a larger star and the centripetal force. For example, Earth is a satellite because it is attracted to the Sun and stays in its orbit. Similarly, the Moon is a satellite because it kept in orbit by the gravitational pull from the Earth.

The Solar System’s planets, and its most likely dwarf planets, are known to be orbited by at least 218 natural satellites or moons [2]. The biggest moons in the Solar System are Earth’s moon, Jupiter’s Galilean moons (Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto), Saturn’s moon Titan, and Neptune’s captured moon Triton [3]. Notice that most moons in our solar system are actually named after mythological characters from a wide variety of cultures and literature!

The Moon

The most important moon (for us) is the Earth’s moon. It is Earth’s only natural satellite and the largest moon in the Solar System relative to the size of its planet [4]. The Moon’s cycle is about 27.3 days [3]. At different times of the months, different parts of the Moon reflect the light of the Sun, changing its appearance from Earth. The change of view from Earth is also called the lunar phase.

The primary effects the Moon have on Earth are tidal effects. The effects are caused by the slightly greater gravitational force the Moon has for the side of Earth closest to it. This results in elevated sea levels called ocean tides, where one of the ocean bulges (high tide) is held in place closest to the Moon, while another such tide (low tide) is opposite [4].

You might already know that Neil Armstrong became the first person to walk on the Moon under the Apollo 11 mission. After that, many more exploration programs have been initiated, mostly for exploring or mining some rare elements [4].

After many years of exploration and experiments, one might ask, “Can we actually live on the Moon?” Well, the short answer is yes! During Apollo 17 mission, astronauts have stayed for days on the Moon [4]. The key idea is to cover the basic human needs, which are air, water, food, and shelter. So, the first task is to make breathable air. As the lunar soil consists of 42% oxygen, this can be done easily by using robots to harvest oxygen on the surface. With that in mind, NASA has already developed and field- tested prototype robots on Earth capable of doing such task [5].

Next on the list is water, which can be compounded from 2/3 hydrogen and 1/3 oxygen. Although we have plenty of oxygen, we unfortunately lack hydrogen. At present, the only option is to ship liquid hydrogen from Earth to the Moon. However, each trip to space is very expensive, so it is possible but not cost- effective [5].

Although we have breathable air and water, astronauts could only last for a few days as the surface of the Moon consists of lunar dust and lots of toxic compounds [4]. The contamination, in turn, poses a danger to health and damages crops. In addition, setting up a permanent camp on the Moon is difficult and much more expensive than sending astronauts there. These challenges already omitted two out of four basic human needs and hence stop us from having a settlement on the Moon.

Artificial Satellites

Usually, the word "satellite" refers to a man-made machine that is launched into space and orbit around Earth or another body in space [1]. Since Sputnik 1 was successfully launched Earth on 4 October 1957, thousands of satellites from more than 40 countries have been launched for various purposes [6]. The main applications for of these satellites include [6]:

  • Earth observation
  • Space exploration
  • Communication satellite
  • Television satellite
  • Navigation satellite
  • Space tourism
  • Protecting Earth from potentially hazardous objects
  • Space colonisation

In everyday life, we probably notice many benefits of these satellites. For example, the Earth observations satellites usually take real-time pictures of the planet, which allows prediction of weather and track natural disasters such as hurricanes [1]. Communication and televisions satellites also employed to send TV and phone signal instantly across different locations on Earth, allowing a smooth communication no matter where you are [6].
