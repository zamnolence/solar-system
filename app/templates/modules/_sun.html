The Sun



Characteristics

The Sun, a yellow dwarf star, is one of more than 100 billion stars in the Milky Way. Though massive, it is still considered a “dwarf” as compared to other types of stars [1]. It is the heart of the Solar System and its gravity keeps every planet and particle in orbit. Its shape is a nearly perfect sphere, comprising about 99.8% of the Solar System’s mass, with diameter roughly 109 times that of Earth [1]. To put it simply, if the Sun were as tall as a typical front door, Earth would be about the size of a nickel!

Three-quarters of the Sun's mass, consisting of about 92.1% hydrogen and 7.8% [1], which typically makes it a ball of gas. Hot plasma in its core is heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions, providing heat, light, and ionization, and through the continuous outflow of a magnetized, supersonic ionised gas known as the solar wind [1].

Space Weather

The term “space weather” refers to the outer space environment affected by light and electrically charged particles and magnetic fields of the Sun. This includes solar events such as solar flares, coronal mass ejections, and solar wind [2].

An outward burst of plasma we see on the Sun’s surface is called solar flares, whereas high-speed streams coming from the coronal holes is solar wind [2]. These solar events usually affect Earth when the flare or wind cloud is aimed at Earth. The well-known impacts are the disruption of telegraph service in 1859 and the collapse of the Hydro-Québec power network in 1989 [2]. This makes forecasting space whether important.

Other than that, Auroras are a notorious example of the impacts of space weather events, which occur most frequently during the most intense phase of the 11-year solar or sunspot cycle [2].

Solar Radiation and its Impact

The Sun is the energy source for life on Earth and is the essential modulator of the human physical environment as we know it. It is also a source of the full spectrum of ultraviolet radiation, UVA, UVB, and UVC [2] . All types of UV radiation are dangerous and can cause sun burn, skin damage and skin cancer. While UVC and UVB are the most dangerous, Ozone absorbs all UVC and most of UVB from reaching the Earth’s surface [3].

At this point, one might asks, “Do astronauts need sunscreen or extra protections in space?” The answer is they do need extra protections. But sunscreen? Not so much. In addition to skin damage, overexposure to ultraviolet light can cause “snow blindness” and other optic conditions, which might lead to temporary blindness [4]. With so much unfiltered UV radiation, all spacesuits and spaceships are designed to protect astronauts from it. For instance, the suits are made of heavy fabrics with ultraviolet-stabilised plastic see- through helmets, and the space vessels’ windows are UV-blocked. Therefore, sunscreen is not really necessary.
